About PiCMR

LPiCMR is pin-camera handiwork designed by Squirrel team. It is an assignment of the course "Computational Photography"(CS189) in Computer Science Department of Dartmouth College.

This work took around 1 week.
First trial: Sept. 22, 2015 Indoor Test
Version 1: Sept. 24, 2015 Outdoor Test
Version 2: Sept. 26, 2015 Outdoor Test & Improvement

Our Camera Model

Welcome to our PiCMR journey!

Pinhole Sizes

PiCMR uses a narrow card with 4 different holes on it(diameters: 0.1mm, 0.5mm, 1mm, 1.5mm), which helps us easily choose the preferred size to take photos.

Version 1

The first version of PiCMR used a cell phone to record the generated image.

Version 2

Version 2 improves the recording effect using a Sony Nex5. Besides, the box is wrapped with aluminum foil to make it light proof.

Work-site

A picture taken by a friend. Working in a ground-level room in Sudicoff.

First Trial

We firstly thought to use my Nex5 to capture the results, yet found my 30mm prime lens could not focus on the inside screen of the box. The depths of box is too small. We had to use a cellphone to perform our first test.

Inside Faces

One inside face of the cardbox is coverred with white paper to serve as the screen; the rest of the faces with black paper to minimize interreflections.

Holes Cutting

Cut a hole on the box and cover it with card paper, and punch the pinholes of varying sizes into the card paper. Next to the hole for the card paper, cut a hole for the digital camera's aperture.

Indoor Test

Test it to see if it can work in front of a lamp in lab. The shadows over the lamp are two of my fingers.

Results by Version 1

Having seen the image in the first test, we believed it could work and wet on to take more pictures. We put a set of brackets on the box to hold the cell phone. However, the performance was not so good as we had thought. The picture was dark if we used the original camera software in the phone, while third-party apps also disappoined us with their strange outcomes.

Sample 1 (1mm, 10s)

Because the cell phone itself at most allowed us to set the shutter to 10s, the picture seemed dark, even we used the highest ISO.

Sample 2 (1.5mm, 10s)

Even we use the 1.5mm pinhole, the image was still dark. We noticed that 10s was not enough to get a clear picture.

Sample 3 (1mm, 30s)

We adjust the shutter speed to 30s using a third-party app. The result was strange. We believed it should not be so vague since the Sample 1 was clear although without enough explosure.

Modifications in Version 2

Knowing a cell phone can not satisfy us, we decided to use a digital lens camera. We replaced the 30mm lens with a 19mm one and it could perfectly focus on the image. We cut a bigger round circle on the box to let the lens in, and did some tests and modifications to improve its performence.
Here lists some failures in the process. The final results will be shown in nect section.

Light Leakage

We found a severe light leakage problem. Obviously the lights came in though the connections of the lens and box.

Red Spot Effect

To make it light proof, we covered the box with aluminum foil. The light leakage was alleviated, while the red light, which has the longest wave length, still snuck into the box.

Foil Shelter

We thus insert more aluminum foil into the connection part. The result was exciting! Unfortunately, some foil blocked the lights which were supposed to reach the screen.
